The interesting problem

You are building on top of two structured knowledge graphs (biology and competitive landscape) and asking an agent to answer the hardest questions in early R&D. Your own blog argues that hallucinations, audit trails, and version control are the must-haves for biotech AI.

The interesting problem is not whether the agent can answer. It is whether the path from question to answer is inspectable enough for a regulator to accept, and cheap enough to maintain as both graphs grow.

The frame

60 / 30 / 10

We think in layers. A rough split on most AI systems: 60% of what looks like an AI problem is traditional code and data structure, 30% is rule-based logic, and 10% is genuine model work.

For a platform like InovaSight, the orchestration question is which layer each piece of context sits on. Knowledge-graph schema, retrieval strategy, prompt assembly, agent planning, evaluation: each has a natural home, and the agent gets interpretable when those homes are named and kept apart. That is what we do with clients.

The relevant case study

We ran a scoped sprint with a product CTO at Feeld. A workshop, a set of advisory calls, an Organizational Context Architecture, and a Strategic Operations Framework sitting on top of their existing AI work.

The output was not a new platform. It was a clearer picture of which of their problems belonged on which layer, and a methodology their team could keep applying after we left. That is the shape of engagement that tends to fit a seed-stage TechBio best.

The relevant paper

Jake's Interpretable Context Methodology paper is public. It argues that agent context can be organized as a layered filesystem (L0 identity through L4 working artifacts) with measurable interpretability and reproducibility gains.

The repo is at github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM- under MIT license.
